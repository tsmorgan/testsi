Self-isolation

When to self-isolate

You should self-isolate immediately if you have coronavirus (COVID-19) or might have the virus.

If you've had the COVID-19 vaccine, you must still self-isolate. This is because it’s not known yet how much the vaccine stops the virus spreading.

Children under 18 years old should tell a trusted adult before self-isolating.

If you think you've been in contact with someone who has coronavirus, but you do not have symptoms and have not been told to self-isolate, continue to follow social distancing advice.

If you have symptoms

Self-isolate immediately if you have coronavirus symptoms:

  • a high temperature
  • a new, continuous cough
  • a loss or change to sense of smell or taste

Get a test as soon as possible to see if you have the virus. The test needs to be done in the first 8 days of having symptoms.

Get a free PCR test to check if you have coronavirus on GOV.UK

You may want to tell people you've been in close contact with in the past 48 hours that you might have coronavirus.

What does close contact mean?

Examples of close contact include:

  • close face to face contact (under 1 metre) for any length of time – including talking to them or coughing on them
  • being within 1 to 2 metres of each other for more than 15 minutes – including travelling in a small vehicle
  • spending lots of time in your home, such as cleaning it

If you’ve tested positive

You should self-isolate immediately if you’ve had a positive test result. This means you have coronavirus.

There’s more information about self-isolating if you get a positive test result.

If someone you live with has coronavirus

Self-isolate immediately if either:

  • someone you live with has coronavirus symptoms
  • someone you live with has tested positive

You could have the virus without knowing it.

If someone in your support or childcare bubble has coronavirus

Self-isolate if either:

  • someone in your support or childcare bubble has symptoms and you’ve been in close contact with them since their symptoms started or during the 48 hours before they started
  • someone in your support or childcare bubble tested positive and you’ve been in close contact with them since they had the test or in the 48 hours before their test

The people you live with do not need to self-isolate.

What’s a support bubble and a childcare bubble?

A support bubble is where someone who lives alone (or just with their children) can meet people from 1 other household. Find out more about making a support bubble with another household on GOV.UK.

A childcare bubble is where 1 household links with one other household to provide informal childcare to anyone under 14. Find out more about making a childcare bubble with another household on GOV.UK.

If you’ve been told to self-isolate by NHS Test and Trace

Self-isolate if you've been told by NHS Test and Trace that you've been in contact with someone who tested positive.

The people you live with do not need to self-isolate.

Find out what to do if you're told to self-isolate by NHS Test and Trace or the NHS COVID-19 app

It's a legal requirement to self-isolate if you are told to by NHS Test and Trace. You could be fined if you do not stay at home.

If you’ve recently returned from abroad

Self-isolate if you enter the UK from outside the Common Travel Area (Ireland, the Channel Islands or the Isle of Man).

See how to quarantine when you arrive in England on GOV.UK.

How long to self-isolate

How long to self-isolate

If you test positive, your self-isolation period includes the day your symptoms started (or the day you had the test, if you do not have symptoms) and the next 10 full days.

Anyone you live with will also need to self-isolate at the same time.

You may need to self-isolate for longer if you get symptoms while self-isolating or your symptoms do not go away.

Read more about how long to self-isolate.
